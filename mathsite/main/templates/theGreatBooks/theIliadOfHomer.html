BOOK I

This story begins by saying there was some great peril that was brought upon the Achaeans. Sending many of them down to Hades due to a consistent plague. The son of Jove, and Leto were the culprits of this. They were mad because the kings of the Achaeans had dishonoured Chryses.

The Achaeans had kidnapped Chryses' daughter, so Chryses traveled to the Achaeans to get her back. By doing so he offered the chiefs of the Achaeans a ransom; specifically "the sceptre Apollo, wreathed with a suppliant's wreath".

Agamemnon heard Chryses' plea to get his daughter back but Agamemnon would not accept his ransom. He told Chryses to leave and never come back, contrary to the rest of the Achaeans who wanted to accept the ransom.

Chryses then prayed to the god Apollo, asking him to "avenge his tears", which I believe meant he wished to have the Achaeans killed, or at least Agamemnon. Apollo answered his prayer, came down from Olympus, and shot the Achaeans' livestock for 9 days. Finally, Achilles, in his confusion, approached Calchas and asked him if he know why the livestock were being killed and why they obtained a plague. Thereon, a seer told Achilles that it was because of the God Apollo and that the only way to stop the plague and the killings was to give Chryses his daughter back. Agamemnon heard this and was furious.

Agamemnon went on to say that he loved the girl more than his wife, but that he would give her away if it saves his people from the plague. However, he said he'd still require a "prize". Achilles said he doesn't need one and there's nothing to give him. Agamemnon agreed to sail away to return Chryses' daughter, but still demanded a prize, this time from Achilles and Achilles now grew angry with Agamemnon for Agamemnon expressed hurtful words towards Achilles.

Now Achilles was furous with Agememnon, he nearly drew his sword in anger when Minerva, goddess of wisdom, came down from the heavens and stopped him. Instead, Achilles simply proceeded to insult Agamemnon back, essentially calling him a coward.

Hector intervened, said Agamemnon should not take the girl away because Achilles will one day become king and replace Agamemnon. Agamemnon agreed that Achilles is a great warrior, but a foolish one. They eventually came to an accord and the Achaeans took Chryses' daughter, Chryseis, on a ship to deliever her back to the Chryses.

Now remember how Agamemnon said he would not give away Chryseis without a prize in return? After not recieving anything, he sent his servants to steal Bresies, a woman living with Achilles, his "true love". Achilles was aware of the theives and let them take Brieis to Agamemnon, but told the gaurds that Agamemnon will regret stealing from "the great Achilles".

Achilles wept to his mother and told her everything that happened. He asked for her to beckon Zeus for help and so she said she'd visit Olympus and ask him to aid Achilles in his time of sorrow.

Meanwhile, the Achaeans arrived to Chryses, lead by Ulysses. They delivered Chryseis, feasted happily, then sailed back to their home. The god Apollo had thus forgiven the Achaeans.

Thetis ended up making it to Olympus, pressed Zeus to do something about Achilles losing Briseis, and Zeus never made a decision. He pondered about it, suggested that he could force the Trojans to attack the Achaeans, but Zeus's wife Juno did not like that idea since she was fond of the Achaeans. The fought the idea for a while, their son The Vulcan calmed them down, they then drank sweet nectar and all went to bed, with Zeus still needing to make a decision about what to do for Achilles.

BOOK II

Zeus thought of how to do honour to Achilles. His conclusion was to give him the kingdom of Troy, but it must be taken over. Thus, he sent a dream to Agamemnon, (King of the Achaeans), which was delivered by Nestor while Agamemnon was sleeping. Agamemnon woke and spoke to his soldiers. He told them to bare arms for they were going to go conquer Troy.

Meanwhile, the goddess Juno, told the goddess Minerva to deliver a message to Ulysses, a message saying they should not fight Troy, for there have been to many Achaean lives lost in the battling Troy in the past. Ulysses went to the kings and captains of the Achaean army and told them to not be so eager in battling Troy, for they don't know the full intent of Agamemnon yet.

Thersites beckoned Agamemnon to not battle Troy. Ulysses called him a coward and beat him. Thersites had changed Ulysses mind about battling Troy. The Achaean people gathered many soldiers and ships and prepared for war. Word got out to the Trojans, and they too gathered all their forces to defend their kingdom.

Book III

The battle between the Achaeans and the Trojans was about to begin. Hector, King of the Trojans, spoke with his brother Alexander (Paris) with an idea. As the battle was about to begin, Hector saw Menelaus approach Alexander in pursuit to kill him. Hector saw Alexander cower. So, Hector's idea was to have Alexander and Menelaus conduct a solo battle to the death. The winner would gain fortunes from the other, along with Helen and her belongings.

The propsition was said to Agamemnon and the Achaeans agreed. Everyone gathered around to witness the duel, including Helen and Alexander's father Priam. Priam decided he couldn't watch the fight because he knew the outcome would result in the death of his son. He fled to Ilius.

The battle began. Paris was losing and all the while Menelaus was praying to Jove as he faught. The goddess Venus, a goddess and daughter of Jove, heard of the battle, flew down from Olympus, and snatched up Paris "(as a god can do)" and brought him to his own bedchamber. The battlefield was left with smoke, thus nobody knew what happened to Paris. They all assumed he had parished or fled, and so Menelaus was deemed the victor. Venus, also took Helen to the bedchamber to join Paris. Helen thought Paris was a coward but she stayed with him all the same.

The Achaeans thought Menelaus had won and demanded Helen and all her wealth.
